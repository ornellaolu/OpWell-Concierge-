Breast Augmentation: Complete Guide to Implants, Surgery & Recovery

By Dr. Ornella Oluwole, MD • Board-Certified Anesthesiologist
Published: March 2024 | Updated: May 2026
Read time: 12-15 minutes

Breast augmentation (augmentation mammaplasty) is one of the most commonly requested cosmetic procedures. Yet many patients have questions about implant types, surgical approaches, safety, and what recovery really looks like. This comprehensive guide covers everything from implant selection and surgical options to recovery timeline, complications, and long-term considerations — from an anesthesiologist's perspective.

Breast Implant Types: Saline vs. Silicone

Saline Implants

What they are: Silicone shell filled with sterile saltwater (saline solution).

Feature Details
FDA Approval Approved for all patients age 18+; first-line option
Feel Slightly firmer than silicone; may feel less natural (depends on patient sensitivity)
Rupture Detection Immediate — saline leaks into body, breast visibly deflates within hours
Safety if Rupture Saline is sterile saltwater; no health risk if leaked. Body reabsorbs fluid.
Cost Usually $1,000–$2,000 less than silicone (per pair)
Longevity Typical lifespan: 15–20 years; 10–20% rupture rate over 10 years
Visibility on Imaging Visible on X-rays, ultrasound, and MRI

Best for: Younger patients, price-conscious patients, those who value rupture detection simplicity

Silicone Implants

What they are: Silicone rubber shell filled with cohesive silicone gel (feels more like breast tissue).

Feature Details
FDA Approval Approved for ages 22+; requires breast reconstruction or prior saline use
Feel Softer, more natural; mimics breast tissue better than saline
Rupture Detection Silent ruptures possible — gel may stay in shell or migrate slowly
Safety if Rupture Gel stays localized (cohesive design). No systemic toxicity. Mostly safe if ruptures, but should be removed.
Cost Usually $1,000–$2,000 more than saline per pair
Longevity Similar to saline: 15–20 years typical; 5–10% rupture rate over 10 years
Monitoring FDA recommends MRI screening every 3 years post-op to detect silent ruptures

Best for: Patients who prioritize natural feel, willing to undergo periodic MRI screening

Implant Safety Record
• Complication rates (saline vs silicone): very similar
• No proven link between silicone and autoimmune disease (studied extensively)
• Both FDA-approved and considered safe when properly monitored
• Patient satisfaction: 80–95% across both types
• Revision surgery needed (10 years): ~25% for both types

Surgical Approaches: Where the Implant Goes

Incision Placement Options

1. Inframammary (Under the Breast)

  • Location: Incision in the natural fold under the breast
  • Advantages: Best visualization for surgeon; easiest access; lowest revision rates
  • Disadvantages: Visible scar (though usually fades); may be visible in swimwear/low-cut tops
  • Best for: First-time augmentation; preferred by most surgeons

2. Periareolar (Around the Areola)

  • Location: Incision along the edge of the nipple/areola
  • Advantages: Scar hidden in natural color transition; good for breast lift + augmentation combined
  • Disadvantages: Slightly higher risk of nipple sensation changes; higher infection risk; harder visualization for surgeon
  • Best for: Patients with larger areola; combination lift + augmentation

3. Axillary (Through the Armpit)

  • Location: Incision in the armpit
  • Advantages: No incision on breast; popular for career-conscious patients
  • Disadvantages: Requires endoscope-assisted placement (more complex); higher rates of malposition; limited if revision needed
  • Best for: Patients highly motivated by scar visibility

Implant Pocket Location

Subglandular (Over the Muscle)

  • Implant placed directly under breast tissue, on top of chest muscle
  • Pros: Faster recovery; less pain; better for athletic patients
  • Cons: More visible rippling; implants may be more palpable

Submuscular (Under the Muscle)

  • Implant placed partially/fully under pectoralis major muscle
  • Pros: More natural appearance; better camouflage of implant; lower capsular contracture risk
  • Cons: Longer recovery (4–6 weeks); more post-op pain; can cause muscle animation in some patients

Dual-plane (Hybrid)

  • Upper half under muscle, lower half under breast tissue
  • Tries to balance benefits of both approaches
  • Most popular option today
Surgical Outcomes by Approach
• Revision surgery rates: Inframammary 10–15%, Periareolar 12–18%, Axillary 15–25%
• Capsular contracture (implant hardening): 5–10% submuscular, 15–20% subglandular
• Patient satisfaction: 85–90% across all approaches

Anesthesia for Breast Augmentation

Anesthesia Options

  • IV Sedation (Twilight): Most common; patient sedated but breathing on own; typical choice
  • General Anesthesia: Full unconsciousness with breathing tube; chosen by some patients or surgeons
  • Local Only: Rarely used (patient awareness of breast manipulation uncomfortable)

Anesthesia Considerations

  • Operative time: 1.5–3 hours (longer if lift combined)
  • Pain control: Moderate post-op pain (adequately managed with medication)
  • Position: Supine (on back) throughout — requires careful padding and positioning
  • Monitoring: Standard (heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen, anesthesia depth)
  • Special consideration: Implant placement requires surgeon precision; patient must be stable and still

Sizing and Selection

How Sizes Are Measured

Implant size is measured in cubic centimeters (cc):

  • Small: 150–300 cc (subtle enhancement)
  • Medium: 300–500 cc (moderate enhancement; most common)
  • Large: 500–800 cc (dramatic enhancement)
  • Extra large: 800+ cc (very prominent; requires strong tissue support)

Factors in Sizing

  • Current breast tissue volume and shape
  • Chest wall width (determines safe implant width)
  • Skin elasticity (affects how well tissue stretches)
  • Patient goals (subtle vs. dramatic enhancement)
  • Implant profile (height of implant — can make same cc look different)
  • Bra cup size desired (very individual; same cc size can equal different cup sizes depending on body)

Important: There's no "standard" way to convert implant cc to bra cup size. A 500 cc implant might be a C cup for one patient, D cup for another. Virtual try-ons and implant sizers help, but exact predictions are impossible.

Breast Augmentation Recovery Timeline

Immediate Post-Op (First 24 Hours)

Pain: 5–7 out of 10 (manageable with prescribed pain medication)
Swelling: Moderate; peaks around 48 hours
Bruising: Minimal initially; develops over first few days
Activity: Rest; light movement only; have someone with you
Bra: Supportive bra or surgical compression provided; wear constantly

Week 1 Post-Op

  • Pain: 3–5 out of 10; most patients stop narcotic pain meds by day 5–7
  • Swelling: Still significant (peaks around day 3–4)
  • Bruising: Visible; yellow/purple bruising normal
  • Activity: Can walk, light stretching; avoid heavy lifting
  • Driving: Once off narcotic pain meds and pain is controlled (<4/10)
  • Work: Can return to desk work by end of week for most patients
  • Showering: Once incision cleared by surgeon (usually 48–72 hours)

Weeks 2–4 Post-Op

  • Pain: 1–3 out of 10; mostly with movement or physical therapy
  • Swelling: Gradually improves; still noticeable
  • Bruising: Fading; yellow/greenish tones as healing progresses
  • Activity: Can resume most daily activities; avoid heavy lifting (>5–10 lbs)
  • Exercise: Light walking OK; no chest exercise or weight training yet
  • Appearance: Breasts feel firm/high initially; will drop and settle over weeks

Months 2–3 Post-Op

  • Pain: Minimal; mostly resolved
  • Swelling: Mostly gone by week 6–8; residual swelling can last 3–6 months
  • Bruising: Should be completely resolved
  • Exercise: Can resume weight training and chest exercises (gradual)
  • Final results: Not fully apparent until 6–12 months (swelling continues to resolve slowly)
  • Implant settling: Breasts continue to drop and soften as scar tissue relaxes

Common Post-Op Sensations (Normal vs. Red Flags)

Normal Post-Op Feelings

  • Breasts feel firm/tight (temporary; will soften)
  • Breasts sit high on chest (temporary; will drop into position)
  • Tightness when lying down (normal; improves with time)
  • Nipple sensation changes (temporary; usually resolves in weeks to months)
  • Pinching/squeezing sensation (temporary; normal healing sensation)
  • Mild numbness around incision (normal; resolves over months)

Red Flags — Call Your Surgeon

⚠️ Contact Your Surgeon Immediately If:
• Fever >101°F — possible infection
• Severe, sudden pain (beyond normal post-op)
• Incision separation or gaping
• Pus, foul smell, or excessive drainage
• Extreme swelling in one breast only
• Implant feels like it's slipped out of position
• Difficulty breathing or chest pain
• Numbness/tingling spreading beyond normal sensation
• Severe bruising beyond normal range

Complications and Risks

Short-Term (First Few Months)

  • Hematoma (bleeding): 1–5%; usually managed conservatively; occasionally requires drainage
  • Seroma (fluid collection): 2–5%; may require drainage if large
  • Infection: 1–3%; treated with antibiotics; rarely requires implant removal
  • Nipple/skin sensation changes: 15–30%; usually temporary (weeks to months); permanent in <5%

Long-Term

  • Capsular contracture: 5–20% (implant hardens from scar tissue); may require reoperation
  • Implant rupture: 1–2% per year (increases over time; cumulative 20–30% at 10 years)
  • Implant malposition: 2–5%; may cause cosmetic issues or pain
  • Bottoming out: Implant drops too low; can occur years post-op
  • Animation: Breast movement with muscle contraction; subjective; bothers some patients
Revision Surgery Statistics
• 1 in 4 patients need revision surgery within 10 years
• Most common reasons: contracture, malposition, size change, implant rupture
• Risk increases with time (more likely at 15+ years than 5 years)
• Revision surgery has lower complication rates than primary surgery

Long-Term Implant Considerations

How Long Do Implants Last?

Implants are NOT lifetime devices. Average lifespan is 15–20 years, though many last longer. Reasons for replacement:

  • Rupture (saline visible immediately; silicone requires MRI to detect)
  • Capsular contracture (scar tissue hardens implant)
  • Patient age/life changes (desire for different size, lift, or removal)
  • Material degradation (extremely rare with modern implants)

Monitoring: What's Required?

  • Saline implants: Self-monitoring only; any rupture is immediately obvious
  • Silicone implants: FDA recommends MRI screening every 3 years to detect silent ruptures
  • Clinical exams: Annual breast exam by surgeon (or annual self-exam + clinical monitoring)
  • Mammography: Standard mammography screening can still be done; implant displacement views recommended

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding After Augmentation

Can you get pregnant after breast augmentation? Yes, safely.

Impact on breastfeeding:

  • Subglandular implants: No impact on milk supply; milk ducts not disrupted
  • Periareolar approach: Small risk of reduced nipple sensation or milk duct disruption
  • Inframammary approach: No impact on breastfeeding; ducts not affected

Pregnancy effects on augmented breasts:

  • Breasts enlarge during pregnancy (tissue growth around implant)
  • Risk of sagging/ptosis post-pregnancy (tissue stretches; implant weight contributes)
  • Nursing is safe; implant materials don't transfer to breast milk

Key Takeaways

  • Saline and silicone are both FDA-approved; choice depends on preference (cost vs. natural feel)
  • Incision placement and implant pocket location significantly affect results and recovery
  • Recovery takes 1–2 weeks for light activity, 4–6 weeks for full exercise clearance, 6–12 months for final results
  • Swelling peaks around 48 hours and gradually improves over 3–6 months
  • Pain is usually manageable; most patients stop narcotic pain meds by day 5–7
  • Implants are not lifelong; revision surgery is common (1 in 4 patients within 10 years)
  • Saline ruptures are immediately obvious; silicone ruptures require MRI screening
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding are safe after augmentation
  • IV sedation is the typical anesthesia choice; general anesthesia is option for some patients
  • Choose an experienced surgeon and board-certified anesthesiologist; safety and expertise matter more than cost

Bottom line: Breast augmentation is a well-established, safe cosmetic procedure with high patient satisfaction. Recovery is straightforward for most patients when pre-operative planning is thorough and post-operative instructions are followed carefully. Choosing qualified providers and realistic expectations about results and longevity ensures the best outcomes.
