What Happens Under General Anesthesia: Complete Safety Guide

By Dr. Ornella Oluwole, MD • Board-Certified Anesthesiologist
Published: March 2024 | Updated: May 2026
Read time: 12-15 minutes

General anesthesia feels mysterious. One moment you're awake on the operating table, the next you're being wheeled to recovery with no memory of surgery. This guide demystifies what actually happens: how anesthesia drugs work, what the anesthesiologist is monitoring, why intubation happens, and what to expect as you wake up. Understanding the "why" behind each step can reduce anxiety and help you prepare confidently.

What Is General Anesthesia?

General anesthesia is a medically induced state of unconsciousness where you cannot feel pain, respond to commands, or remember the surgery. It involves three key components:

  • Hypnosis: Medications to make you sleep (propofol, etomidate, ketamine)
  • Analgesia: Medications to block pain (opioids like remifentanil, morphine)
  • Muscle Relaxation: Medications to paralyze muscles if the surgery requires it (only when intubated; see below)

The anesthesiologist carefully balances these three components throughout surgery, adjusting doses based on your vital signs and surgical needs. It's not a single injection — it's a continuous, dynamic process.

Before Surgery: Pre-Anesthesia Assessment

The Pre-Op Visit (Days or Hours Before Surgery)

Your anesthesiologist will review your:

  • Medical history: Heart disease, lung disease, diabetes, sleep apnea, past anesthesia experiences
  • Medications: Everything you take, including supplements (some interact with anesthesia)
  • Allergies: Especially to anesthesia drugs or latex
  • Airway anatomy: Jaw size, neck mobility, loose teeth, or previous difficult intubations
  • NPO status: "Nothing by mouth" — typically 6–8 hours before surgery to prevent vomiting
Why NPO (Nothing by Mouth)?
• Food/drink in stomach increases aspiration risk (material entering lungs)
• Risk is low with modern anesthesia but serious if it occurs
• General guideline: Nothing after midnight if surgery is morning; 2–6 hours if afternoon surgery
• Follow your specific surgeon's NPO instructions

Airway Assessment

The anesthesiologist examines your jaw, teeth, and neck to predict whether intubation will be straightforward. Certain features (small jaw, neck stiffness, large tongue) may require special equipment or techniques. This assessment is routine and critical for safety.

In the Operating Room: Induction to Unconsciousness

Step 1: IV Line Placement

An IV catheter is placed in your arm (usually before you meet the anesthesiologist). This is your "lifeline" for:

  • Anesthesia medications
  • Emergency medications if needed
  • Fluids to maintain blood pressure
  • Blood products if significant bleeding occurs

Step 2: Pre-Oxygenation (2–3 minutes)

You'll breathe 100% oxygen through a mask. This fills your lungs with oxygen so if there's any delay getting you a breathing tube, you have a cushion of oxygen to maintain safe oxygen levels.

Step 3: Induction — The "Going Under" Moment

An induction agent (usually propofol) is pushed through your IV. Within 30–60 seconds, you'll feel:

  • Warmth in your arm
  • Sudden drowsiness
  • Loss of consciousness

You will not remember anything after this moment until after surgery. The transition is smooth and peaceful — no struggling, no pain.

⚠️ Propofol Injection Sensation:
Some patients report a burning sensation in their IV arm when propofol is injected. This is normal and lasts only seconds. Tell your anesthesiologist beforehand if you're concerned — they can administer it slowly or give pain relief first.

Step 4: Intubation (If Needed)

Once you're unconscious, the anesthesiologist may place a breathing tube (endotracheal tube) down your trachea. This serves several purposes:

  • Airway protection: Prevents saliva, blood, or stomach contents from entering your lungs
  • Controlled ventilation: Machine-controlled breathing ensures optimal oxygen and CO2 levels
  • Muscle paralysis delivery: If the surgery requires complete stillness, paralytic drugs are safer with a secured airway

When Is Intubation Used?

  • Always: Chest surgery, head/neck surgery, emergency surgery
  • Usually: Abdominal surgery, bariatric surgery, longer cases (>2 hours)
  • Sometimes: Some cosmetic surgeries, shorter procedures (anesthesiologist's judgment)
  • Rarely: Minor procedures under light sedation (IV anesthesia without a tube)

After intubation: If muscle relaxants are used, you'll be temporarily paralyzed (unable to move) but completely unconscious. The ventilator breathes for you. The anesthesiologist monitors your oxygen, CO2, and depth of anesthesia continuously.

Intubation Safety Data
• Successfully placed on first attempt in 95%+ of cases
• Sore throat post-op occurs in 40–90% of intubated patients (resolves in days)
• Vocal cord injury is rare (<1% of cases) and usually temporary
• Awareness during anesthesia is extremely rare (1–2 per 10,000)

During Surgery: Anesthesia Monitoring

While you're unconscious, the anesthesiologist continuously monitors multiple vital signs and adjusts medications to keep you safe. Here's what's being watched:

Heart & Blood Pressure Monitoring

  • ECG (electrocardiogram): 5–10 electrode pads on your chest measure heart rate and rhythm
  • Blood pressure cuff: Automatically inflates every 3–5 minutes (or continuously if arterial line placed)
  • Pulse oximetry: Small clip on your finger (or ear) measures oxygen saturation — target >95%

Breathing Monitoring

  • Capnography: Measures CO2 in your exhaled breath — confirms intubation placement and adequate ventilation
  • Oxygen concentration: The ventilator delivers a set oxygen percentage (usually 50–100% depending on surgery)
  • Airway pressures: Monitors resistance in the breathing tube and lungs (detects obstruction or lung changes)

Anesthesia Depth Monitoring

  • BIS Monitor (Bispectral Index): Electrode on your forehead measures brain electrical activity to assess anesthesia depth (0 = unconscious, 100 = awake). Target 40–60 during surgery.
  • Blood pressure & heart rate: Sudden increases may suggest inadequate anesthesia
  • Movement or muscle tone: If you're moving or straining, anesthesia needs adjustment

Temperature Management

  • Core temperature probe: Usually in the esophagus or bladder
  • Warming blanket: Anesthesia causes heat loss; warmers prevent hypothermia
  • Target: Maintain 35.5–37.5°C (96–99.5°F)

Other Monitoring

  • Neuromuscular monitoring: If paralytic drugs used, small electrical stimulation on your arm confirms the paralysis level
  • Fluid status: IV input/output monitoring ensures adequate hydration
  • Urine output: Foley catheter collects urine to assess kidney perfusion

Anesthesia Medications: What's Being Given

Induction Agents (One-Time at Start)

  • Propofol: Most common; smooth, rapid onset; can cause low blood pressure
  • Etomidate: Used if blood pressure is low; rapid onset; minimal cardiovascular effects
  • Ketamine: Used for trauma or high-risk patients; maintains blood pressure; can cause hallucinations on wake-up

Maintenance Agents (Continuous During Surgery)

  • Inhaled gases (Sevoflurane, Isoflurane): Sweet-smelling gas mixed with oxygen; dose adjusted throughout surgery
  • IV infusions (Propofol, Dexmedetomidine): Continuous IV drips instead of gas; often used with regional anesthesia

Pain Control (Opioids)

  • Remifentanil: Ultra-short acting; adjusts minute-by-minute to surgical stimulation
  • Morphine: Longer lasting; given during surgery and in recovery for post-op pain
  • Fentanyl: 100x more potent than morphine; careful dosing required

Muscle Relaxants (Only If Intubated)

  • Succinylcholine: Ultra-short acting; used for rapid intubation (emergency situations)
  • Cisatracurium, Rocuronium: Intermediate acting; provide sustained paralysis for surgery; reversed before wake-up

Important: At the end of surgery, the anesthesiologist reverses the paralytic drug (if used) and begins waking you up by stopping or reducing anesthesia medications.

The Surgery: What You're Not Aware Of

While you're completely unconscious, a lot is happening:

Induction to first incision: 10–30 minutes for prep, positioning, and final checks
Surgical time: Varies by procedure (cosmetic: 1–3 hours; complex: 5+ hours)
Blood loss monitoring: Anesthesiologist tracks fluids, blood products, and hemodynamic status
Position changes: You may be moved (on your side, back, prone) for surgical access — carefully done to avoid nerve damage
Comfortableness: Padding under shoulders, knees, elbows prevents pressure sores and nerve compression
Medications adjusted: Every 15–30 minutes, anesthesia depth, pain control, and blood pressure are fine-tuned

Waking Up: Emergence From Anesthesia

The Transition

In the final 5–10 minutes of surgery, the anesthesiologist stops anesthesia medications and begins the emergence process:

  • Anesthesia gas is turned off (or IV drip is stopped)
  • Opioids and other drugs are reduced
  • If paralytic was used, it's reversed with a reversal agent
  • Your breathing gradually becomes spontaneous again
  • Your brain electrical activity gradually increases

In the Recovery Room

You'll wake in the recovery room (PACU — Post-Anesthesia Care Unit) usually within 5–10 minutes. You may experience:

  • Grogginess, confusion (completely normal)
  • Slurred speech (anesthesia effects wearing off)
  • Cold (due to body cooling during surgery)
  • Thirst (dry mouth from airway management)
  • Pain at incision site (pain relief is available)
  • Nausea/dizziness (in 20–30% of patients; anti-nausea medications help)
  • No memory of recovery (short-term memory loss is normal for 30+ minutes)
Common Recovery Experiences
• Average time in PACU: 45–60 minutes
• 70% of patients feel reasonably alert after 1 hour
• Full cognitive recovery takes 24 hours (avoid driving, operating machinery)
• Nausea/dizziness resolves in first 1–2 hours post-op with medication

Post-Op Monitoring

The recovery team continuously monitors:

  • Vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen, temperature)
  • Pain level (rated 0–10; pain medication given as needed)
  • Nausea/vomiting (anti-nausea medication if needed)
  • Incision (for bleeding or swelling)
  • Alertness and orientation
  • Once you're stable and alert, you're discharged to your hospital room or, for outpatient surgery, sent home

Common Anesthesia Concerns & Questions

Will I feel pain during surgery?

A: No. You'll be unconscious and receive pain medications (opioids). Even if you had minimal anesthesia, you'd receive deep regional anesthesia (nerve blocks) or local anesthesia at the surgical site. You will not feel pain.

What if I wake up during surgery?

A: Anesthesia awareness is rare (1–2 per 10,000 cases). Modern monitoring with BIS machines makes it even rarer. If you're concerned, tell your anesthesiologist — they can increase anesthesia depth if needed.

Can anesthesia cause permanent damage?

A: Serious permanent damage from anesthesia alone is extremely rare. Temporary issues (sore throat, muscle soreness from position, bruising) are common but resolve within days. Your anesthesiologist's main focus is preventing adverse events.

Why do I have a sore throat after?

A: If intubated, the breathing tube passes through your vocal cords, causing irritation. This happens in 40–90% of intubated patients and usually resolves in 3–7 days. Throat lozenges and hydration help.

Is it okay to be nervous about anesthesia?

A: Yes, and it's very common. A pre-operative anesthesia consultation specifically addresses your fears, explains the process, and creates a personalized plan. Many patients feel dramatically more confident after this conversation.

Anesthesia Safety: The Bottom Line

Modern Anesthesia Safety Record
• Mortality directly related to anesthesia: 1 per 100,000 healthy patients
• Serious complications: 2–3 per 10,000
• Most complications are preventable with good pre-operative planning
• Anesthesia today is safer than driving a car

General anesthesia is one of modern medicine's greatest achievements. It allows surgeons to perform complex procedures while you remain completely unconscious and pain-free. The anesthesiologist is a specialist in keeping you safe, comfortable, and aware throughout the process.

Key Takeaways

  • General anesthesia involves three components: sleep, pain relief, and muscle relaxation
  • You're monitored continuously during surgery — heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen, brain activity, and more
  • Intubation (breathing tube) protects your airway and allows controlled breathing
  • Multiple medications work together — you don't feel pain, you stay unconscious, and your vital signs stay stable
  • Waking up is gradual; grogginess and confusion are normal
  • Recovery takes about 1 hour in the immediate post-op period; full cognitive recovery takes 24 hours
  • Anesthesia is remarkably safe when overseen by a board-certified anesthesiologist

Bottom line: General anesthesia is a carefully orchestrated medical process designed to keep you safe, unconscious, and pain-free while the surgeon does their work. You're not "asleep" — you're in a medically induced state monitored continuously with technology and expertise. Understanding what happens helps demystify the experience and reduces anxiety.
