Brazilian Butt Lift: Complete Safety Guide, Complications & Surgeon Selection

By Dr. Ornella Oluwole, MD • Board-Certified Anesthesiologist
Published: March 2024 | Updated: May 2026
Read time: 12-15 minutes

The Brazilian Butt Lift (BBL) has become one of the fastest-growing cosmetic procedures worldwide — with demand increasing 40% in the last 4 years alone. Yet it remains the subject of significant safety concerns. This comprehensive guide covers what you need to know about BBL risks, complications, recovery, and how to choose a surgeon who prioritizes your safety.

What Is a Brazilian Butt Lift?

A BBL is a body contouring procedure that uses autologous fat transfer — meaning your own fat — to augment and reshape the buttocks. The process has two components:

  1. Fat harvesting: Liposuction removes fat from areas like the abdomen, flanks, and lower back
  2. Fat injection: The harvested fat is processed and strategically re-injected into the gluteal region

The dual benefit is that you simultaneously slim donor areas while enhancing buttock volume and shape. Results typically stabilize after 3–6 months as swelling resolves and the graft settles.

86% Patient Satisfaction
A large series of over 3,000 patients reported high satisfaction rates, with approximately 70% of transferred fat surviving long-term in the buttocks.

Why Is BBL So Popular?

Gluteal augmentation is one of the fastest-growing aesthetic procedures worldwide. The typical BBL patient is female (96.7%), with a mean age of 42 years. Social media and evolving beauty standards are major drivers of demand, but it's critical to understand the risks before deciding if BBL is right for you.

The Most Serious Risk: Pulmonary Fat Embolism

When discussing BBL safety, we must start with the most serious complication: pulmonary fat embolism (PFE). This occurs when injected fat enters the gluteal blood vessels and travels to the lungs. PFE is the leading cause of BBL-related death.

⚠️ Critical Safety Fact: The mortality rate has improved significantly with modern safety techniques — from approximately 1 in 3,000 cases to about 1 in 15,000 with current best practices. This is comparable to abdominoplasty mortality, but the risk still exists.

The American Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgeons (ASAPS) Task Force found that:

  • 3% of surveyed surgeons had experienced a patient death from BBL
  • 7% had at least one nonfatal PFE complication
  • The majority of deaths occurred in Florida, with a pattern suggesting surgeon fatigue as a contributing factor (most deaths at end of week)

Complete Complication Profile: What Patients Experience

Beyond PFE, BBL carries other risks. A 2025 meta-analysis reviewing data from 22,151 patients found:

Minor Complications (3.58% overall)

  • Seroma (fluid collection): ~2%
  • Contour irregularities: 2.29%
  • Skin necrosis and fat necrosis: rare but reportable
  • Hematoma (bruising/bleeding): managed conservatively

Hospital-Requiring Complications

  • Wound infection: most common complication requiring hospital care (22.3% of patients who experienced any complication)
  • Pulmonary embolism (blood clot): 0.04%
  • Sepsis: rare but serious
Complication Outcomes
Among patients who experienced complications:
• 35.9% required a hospital visit
• 12.6% needed reoperation
These numbers underscore the importance of choosing a surgeon with expertise in managing complications.

How Modern Safety Advances Reduce Risk

The good news: several evidence-based techniques have significantly improved BBL safety. Responsible surgeons use:

1. Subcutaneous-Only Fat Injection (The Single Most Important Safety Measure)

Fat is injected only into the subcutaneous layer (under the skin), never into the gluteal muscle. This is associated with the lowest complication rates and reduces PFE risk.

2. Intraoperative Ultrasound Guidance

Ultrasound allows the surgeon to visualize the cannula in real time, confirming fat is placed in the correct plane. Studies show this is associated with:

  • Lower major complications: 0.02% vs. 0.08% (without ultrasound)
  • Lower minor complications: 2.82% vs. 3.70% (without ultrasound)

3. Appropriate Cannula Size and Technique

Surgeons use cannulas ≥4 mm in diameter and avoid downward cannula angulation, which can increase the risk of injecting into vessels.

4. Limiting Cases Per Day

The ASAPS Working Group recommends a maximum of 3 BBL cases per day. Surgeon fatigue is a documented risk factor — the pattern of deaths occurring at the end of the week supports this.

5. Accredited Surgical Facilities

Procedures should be performed in accredited facilities (AAAASF/QUAD A accredited) with proper monitoring, emergency protocols, and recovery staff trained in BBL complications.

Long-Term Results: What to Expect

Understanding realistic outcomes helps set proper expectations:

Fat Survival

  • Approximately 70% of transferred fat survives long-term
  • A prospective ultrasound study confirmed subcutaneous-only grafting retains an average of 82% of injected fat thickness at 12 months
  • Some volume loss (approximately 18% reduction) occurs in the first 12 months as the graft stabilizes

Recovery Timeline

  • Weeks 1–2: Expect significant swelling, bruising, and discomfort. Sitting directly on the buttocks is restricted.
  • Weeks 2–3: Begin returning to light activities. Continue wearing compression garments.
  • Weeks 6–8: Most patients can resume full activity and exercise.
  • 3–6 months: Final results are visible as swelling fully resolves and fat grafts stabilize.

Who Is a Good Candidate for BBL?

Not everyone is an ideal BBL candidate. Consider:

  • Overall health: You should be in good general health with no uncontrolled medical conditions
  • Adequate donor fat: You must have sufficient fat available for liposuction and grafting
  • Stable weight: Major weight fluctuations after surgery can compromise results
  • Non-smoker: Smoking impairs fat graft survival and wound healing (should quit 2+ weeks before and after surgery)
  • BMI: Typical candidates have BMI in the normal to mildly overweight range (mean in large studies: 24.8–28.3 kg/m²)
  • Realistic expectations: The procedure enhances shape and proportion — it is not just about size increase

Red Flags & Green Flags: Choosing Your Surgeon

Surgeon selection is the single most important factor in determining both safety and satisfaction.

✓ Green Flags — What to Look For

  • Board Certification: Certified by the American Board of Plastic Surgery (ABPS) — the only ABMS-recognized board. Do not confuse with "ABCS" (American Board of Cosmetic Surgery), which is NOT ABMS-recognized.
  • Experience: Years in practice with specific expertise in BBL; ask how many procedures they've performed
  • Accredited Facility: AAAASF (QUAD A) accredited surgical center
  • Safety Technique: Uses ultrasound guidance and subcutaneous-only injection method
  • Transparent Communication: Discusses realistic expectations, all risks, and has a clear complication management plan
  • Case Limit: Performs a maximum of 3 BBL procedures per day

⚠️ Red Flags — What to Avoid

  • "Board-certified cosmetic surgeons": ABCS diplomates (not ABPS) have significantly higher rates of state medical board disciplinary actions (9.0% vs. 3.1% for ABPS certified surgeons)
  • Misleading credentials: 69.5% of non-ABPS surgeons use "plastic surgeon" in online advertising despite lacking accredited plastic surgery training
  • Bargain pricing: Suspiciously low costs may indicate corner-cutting on safety protocols
  • High-volume "factory" clinics: Surgeons performing 5+ BBL cases per day are fatigued and at higher risk
  • Pressure to book quickly: Responsible surgeons encourage informed decision-making, not rushed booking
  • No discussion of complications: Any surgeon who downplays risks or won't discuss management protocols is a red flag

Questions to Ask Your Surgeon

Before committing, ask these essential questions:

  1. Are you certified by the American Board of Plastic Surgery (ABPS)?
  2. Is this facility AAAASF (QUAD A) accredited?
  3. Do you use ultrasound guidance during fat injection?
  4. Do you inject fat only into the subcutaneous plane (not the muscle)?
  5. How many BBL procedures have you personally performed?
  6. What is your personal complication rate?
  7. What is your plan if a complication occurs?
  8. How many BBL cases do you perform per day?

The Dangers of Medical Tourism for BBL

Seeking BBL at unregulated facilities or via medical tourism carries significant risks that extend beyond the operating room.

⚠️ Critical Warning: The average cost to treat complications from cosmetic tourism is $26,657 per patient — with some cases exceeding $154,700. This far outweighs any initial savings.

Documented Risks of Medical Tourism

  • Infection: Most common complication, often involving antibiotic-resistant organisms (Mycobacterium species, CRE, Candida auris)
  • Outbreaks documented: Nontuberculous mycobacterial surgical site infections have been linked to cosmetic surgery procedures in the Dominican Republic
  • Blood-borne infections: Patients are at risk of hepatitis B/C, HIV, and other infections from inadequate sterilization
  • Inadequate follow-up: Air travel after surgery increases blood clot risk, and overseas patients often lack local continuity of care
  • Regulatory gaps: Unregulated facilities operate without oversight, accreditation, or accountability

The CDC and major surgical societies (BAAPS, ASAPS, ISAPS) all warn against medical tourism for aesthetic procedures, particularly BBL.

Key Takeaways for Prospective BBL Patients

  • BBL is a popular and generally safe procedure when performed by a qualified, experienced surgeon using evidence-based safety protocols
  • The most serious risk is pulmonary fat embolism (PFE); modern techniques have reduced mortality to approximately 1 in 15,000 cases
  • Minor complications (seroma, irregularities) occur in about 3.6% of cases; hospital-requiring complications in about 35.9% of those who experience any complication
  • Subcutaneous-only injection with ultrasound guidance are the single most important safety measures
  • Surgeon selection is critical: choose ABPS board-certified surgeons at accredited facilities who use ultrasound guidance and limit cases to 3 per day
  • Ask detailed questions about the surgeon's training, experience, complication rates, and safety protocols
  • Avoid medical tourism; unregulated facilities carry significantly higher infection and complication risks
  • Expect 2–3 weeks of restricted sitting, 6–8 weeks before full activity, and final results at 3–6 months
  • Work with your surgeon preoperatively to optimize nutrition, fitness, and smoking status to improve graft survival
