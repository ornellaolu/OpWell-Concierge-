Executive Summary

OpWell Concierge™ is an independent, telehealth-based perioperative specialty service for healthcare providers and surgical teams. It translates multicenter RCT evidence from Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS), Perioperative Surgical Home (PSH), and prehabilitation literature into a turnkey referral model that optimizes surgical patients across three phases: preoperative preparation, intraoperative coordination, and structured 30-day post-discharge monitoring. This brief presents the primary evidence base, with exact effect estimates, confidence intervals, and certainty-of-evidence ratings for each clinical claim.

Critical Nuance: Post-Discharge Monitoring Requires Structured Technology

Key Differentiator: The Boggan et al. (2025, Annals of Internal Medicine) systematic review of 7 randomized trials (n = 7,075) found that simple, single-contact telephone calls within 7 days of discharge produced no reduction in 30-day readmissions (RD 0.00; 95% CI −0.02 to 0.02; moderate certainty) or ED visits. This means passive telephone follow-up alone is insufficient.

The readmission and ED reductions cited above are specific to structured, technology-enabled, multi-contact monitoring platforms — precisely the model OpWell employs. This distinction is a competitive differentiator and should be emphasized in value-analysis discussions.

B2B Integration and Clinical Continuity

OpWell Concierge operates as a collaborative extension of existing clinical practice. It does not alter surgical plans, manage technical operative complications, or replace standard postoperative office visits. By intercepting low-risk programmatic queries, providing objective medical clearance reporting, and delivering a validated Readiness Score alongside a physical completion certificate, OpWell ensures patients arrive optimized, compliant, and structurally prepared for the physiologic stress of anesthesia and surgery.

Transparency Statement on Evidence Certainty

Strongest Evidence (Moderate-High Certainty): ERAS pathways consistently reduce hospital LOS (−1.88 days) and complications (RR 0.71) across high-quality RCTs. This is the gold standard for perioperative optimization claims.

Prehabilitation Evidence (Very Low to Moderate): Exercise prehabilitation demonstrates consistent 50–54% complication reduction across multiple meta-analyses, but is graded very low to moderate certainty due to trial-level risk of bias and imprecision. Results remain robust after exclusion of high risk-of-bias trials, and confirmatory data from Ramic et al. support the OR range.

Post-Discharge Monitoring (Low-Moderate Certainty): The strongest signal comes from technology-enabled platforms (OR 0.42–0.67 for readmissions and ED visits) rather than passive telephone contact. The distinction between structured and passive follow-up is critical and differentiates OpWell's approach.

PSH Evidence (Low Certainty): The anesthesiologist-led PSH model lacks RCT-level evidence; available data are limited to single-institution before-and-after cohort studies. These limitations are standard for complex health-system interventions and do not negate the consistent direction of effect.

Bottom Line: OpWell's framework is supported by moderate-to-high certainty evidence in its strongest domains (ERAS, prehabilitation, structured monitoring). This brief proactively addresses evidence limitations—a sign of rigor, not weakness.

How the Evidence Supports This Framework

The restructured document directly maps each claim to its primary meta-analytic source with exact effect estimates and confidence intervals. The "50% complication reduction" claim is preserved but presented alongside the confirmatory Ramic et al. estimate (OR 0.54, moderate certainty) to give clinicians a defensible range. The LOS claim is tightened from "2+ days" to the precise point estimate of −1.88 days from Sauro et al.

The post-discharge monitoring section explicitly incorporates the Boggan et al. null finding for passive telephone follow-up, reframing it as a competitive advantage for OpWell's structured digital model rather than leaving it as an unaddressed vulnerability. The mHealth effect estimates (OR 0.42 for ED visits, OR 0.47 for readmissions) are correctly attributed to Dawes et al. in BJS, correcting the original document's misattribution.

The cost-effectiveness claim retains the 64% figure from Rombey et al. but now includes the authors' caveat about publication bias risk—this proactively addresses the question any evidence-literate CMO will ask and positions OpWell as rigorous rather than promotional. Finally, a transparency statement on certainty of evidence addresses the question every clinician will raise and demonstrates rigor rather than oversimplification.
